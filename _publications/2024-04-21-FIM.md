The periodic review and evaluation of a dam's performance, especially under the new FERC guidelines, requires an owner to be more detailed in describing the dam's actual performance range and limits. Historically, performance limits have been based on measures of a dam's condition through pressure, leakage, or survey data; all used in conjunction with numerical models to establish the onset of nonlinear behavior. Although a valid approach, owners struggle with the notion that a dam which spends the majority of its service life in a linear state would transition into an irreversible, non-linear, even damaged state under extreme loading conditions. The need exists for establishing field monitoring techniques that capture the dam's performance during actual operating conditions, namely spilling.

Flow-Induced Monitoring (FIM) is a recently developed procedure that uses measured responses on a dam during spilling to determine the dam's current condition, performance levels during spilling, and can be used to develop models for predicting behavior under larger operating loads. Additional functions of FIM include: establishing baseline levels of response during spilling, describing the dam's movements during spilling, and producing indicators for detecting the onset of changing conditions in the dam. The practical implementation of FIM is described at six different projects, along with findings and considerations for integrating FIM into an owner's existing surveillance and monitoring program.
