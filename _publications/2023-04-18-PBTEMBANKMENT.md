Current monitoring and surveillance procedures of an embankment depend on tracking seepage,
discharges, and movements in the embankment. Currently, a reliable field-based technique does
not exist that can characterize the dynamic behavior of an embankment and that produces
measured behavior that can be used to validate models of the embankment. An understanding of
an embankment’s linear behavior can offer insights into the development of simplified modeling
procedures, procedures for long-term monitoring, and for the evaluation of changing conditions
that may arise under both normal and hazard loading conditions.

Performance-Based Testing (PBT) is a non-destructive approach that excites the broadband
response of a system to provide insights into a system’s characteristics. The technique is well established in the evaluation of concrete dams but has not previously been applied to embankments. The technique’s ability to input excitations across a broadband frequency range
can produce measured behavior in an embankment that can be used to describe its linear
condition.

This paper describes the development of a procedure that adapts PBT to an embankment section
located in Mossyrock, WA, leveraging techniques developed for Bouquet Dam in the 1960s.
Details of sensor placement, loading conditions, induced transient behavior, and an evaluation of
the relationships within the embankment are discussed.
